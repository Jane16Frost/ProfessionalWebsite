Robotic Design of Kids Toys

Written by Jane16Frost

1.0 Introduction

Robots are intelligent objects and a way to enhance kids' toys. The design of children's toys that can move, react and think bring a new realm of possibilities to explore. Robots are a new way to design toys for children of different ages, learning difficulties, and needs.

1.1 Purpose

The purpose of this report is to discuss the beneficial effects robotic toys can have. This paper will look at designing for an age group and making inclusive toys. Then examine the social, physical, and educational needs robots were intended to fill.

1.2 Scope

The report will start by laying the groundwork for design elements. Then this report will explain the importance of designing for age groups. Next, it will look at the benefits of inclusive design. Finally, it will dive into robots designed to increase kids’ social skills, activity levels, and skill development. The research will focus on child development and how toys impact it. The paper will limit the research to robotic toys designed to benefit children.

2.0 Toy Design Elements

From a thought to a market-ready product, designers make thousands of choices. A toy is an object that children under the age of 14 use to learn or play with (Government of Canada, 2021). Robots are sensors, motors, and circuit boards that intelligently react to their environment (Encyclopedia, N.D.). Combining the two produces an object capable of moving and responding to a child as they play with or learn from it. The combination creates a toy that can react to the child.

By looking at child development specialists, designers can understand what age groups are physically and cognitively ready for the type of toy (Toy Industry Association, Inc, 2014). Specifically, the child's age impacts their reaction to the toy, as younger kids lack the ability to understand robots.

A toy's design is also heavily influenced by marketability and cost-effectiveness (Toy Industry Association, Inc, 2014). Both are significant factors to consider as they are deciding factors for a toy getting on shelves. Robotic toys tend to be more expensive, and creating a toy that fills specific niches can help to justify that cost.

Robots have benefitted many problems in child development. Exploring the robotic toys designed to socialize, get kids more active, and educate, will demonstrate how. Creating a toy to fill a specific niche leads to unique toy ideas. The individuality of a toy is another crucial design element (Toy Industry Association, Inc, 2014). Looking for problems a toy can solve increases the chance of an original idea.

3.0 Designing for Differences

A significant consideration for toy design comes from demographics. Not all kids are the same. The term kid spans many years of growth and learning. Each stage in child development focuses on different aspects of becoming a functioning adult. Babies and toddlers can lack the understanding required to interact with a robot's complex movements, possibly even becoming frightened. In comparison, older kids will be able to navigate the toy with ease.

Kids with disabilities also have different requirements for their toys. Since disabilities can affect children's development in many ways, robotic toys can be developed to strengthen these skills. Through inclusive design, toys built for one disability can positively affect another. The importance of designing for differences comes from understanding when children are different and when inclusion benefits everyone.

3.1 Designing for Age Groups

Babies to big kids all play with toys, but baby toys and kids' toys look very different. As a child grows, they develop various skills and identities. From the moment a child is born to about nine months, they learn to move their muscles. From there, until about three years old, the child is refining muscle control and forming an understanding of how the world works (Ben-Joseph, 2018). Children’s primary focus and interest revolve around learning to grasp, crawl, walk and run. Their attention is on how things move and why everything is happening. While robotic toys for babies and toddlers are not impossible, children of this age have difficulties understanding robots and their movements (Fink et al., 2014). A baby's lack of understanding of emotions and patterns creates difficulty in figuring out why and how a robot is moving. Furby is an excellent example of a toy with an age limit. Having the ability to change personalities depending on how a child treats it, Furby can freak out children. One mom’s story describes how Furby developed a mean personality and left her five-year-old daughter crying (Christina, 2015).

At four to six years of age, children learn to use robotic toys. They also develop their imagination and social skills (Ben-Joseph, 2018). At this age, their manipulation of robots tends to be limited to if I-push-a-button-the robot does this. As seen with the Furby example, having a robot react to the child’s behaviour requires more cognitive understanding. A child's lack of understanding makes build-your-own robot kits and learn-to-code toys great ideas for this age group. Parents tend to focus on developing their preschoolers' math and linguistic skills, which makes educational toys highly sought after (Parents Editors, 2022).

From six years on, kids start developing their sense of identity and interests (Ben-Joseph, 2018). This age group has many possibilities for toys since the child is forming their interests and have the understanding to quickly learn how a robotic toy works. Social robots that react based on a child's behaviour are great to introduce at these ages.

The potential for a robotic toy to grow with a child is a unique design feature. When coded intentionally, a robot can sing nursery rhymes to a younger child and play games with an older one (Matheson, 2019). Increasing a toy's longevity by providing the correct type of play for an age group can expand the usefulness and desire to buy the toy.

Understanding what age group or range of age groups a toy is for can benefit the marketability of the toy. Each age group has different ways, advertising reaches them. Defining the demographics of a toy can help ensure it reaches the right little hands and bring the most joy to the user.

3.2 Designing for Special Needs

As a child grows and develops, they will learn crucial skills which disabilities can impact in various ways. There are five core skills a child needs to develop. Motor skills are the child's control over their muscles, separated into gross and fine. Linguistic skills are the ability for them to talk and communicate. Cognitive development is their ability to think and understand their environment. Finally, socio-emotional skills are a child's ability to read their own and others’ emotions (Symbiosis Institute of Design, 2022). Numerous mental and physical conditions can delay these core skills(Symbiosis Institute of Design, 2022).

Examining blindness as an example of a physical condition demonstrates how it affects children’s development. Visually impaired kids can struggle with their cognitive, motor and social skills due to a lack of stimulation (Strategic Design Research Journal, 2020). The absence of inclusive toys on the market causes this lack of stimulation. An inclusive toy is any toy able to include everyone and is played with similarly by children with or without a disability (Strategic Design Research Journal, 2020). In the study of making an inclusive toy, researchers found that tactile and auditory stimulation are heavily relied on in blind children. (Strategic Design Research Journal, 2020). This discovery led to the design of the toy found in figure 1.

Figure 1: Toy designed for inclusivity

Note:(Strategic Design Research Journal, 2020)

The toy is designed to have a variety of textures, including wood, various fabrics, velcro and foam. The different textures stimulate the kids’ tactile perception (Strategic Design Research Journal, 2020). The design of this toy can also help develop sighted kids' fine motor skills, which have been found to improve by touching different textures (Symbiosis Institute of Design, 2022). In general, a toy similar to Figure 1 can improve skills in both blind children and kids with dyspraxia who struggle with their motor skills (Symbiosis Institute of Design, 2022). The ability of an inclusive toy is powerful since a feature designed for one disability can positively benefit another. In the design of a robotic toy, adding different textures, sounds and ranges of motion will make a more enjoyable toy and benefit children with disabilities.

4.0 Toys that Serve a Purpose

Toys can serve a variety of purposes. While some toys can be just for fun, a robotic toy can fill a niche. Robots have provided social interaction and improved mental health. Through social robots, children have developed the socio-emotional skills crucial to their development. Implementing a computer brain allows toys to almost come alive and be seen as a thinking and feeling entity. Robotic toys also encourage physical activity by creating new ways to play and adding extra encouragement. Similarly, robots can teach more advanced skills than the core developmental ones. A child can be encouraged by a robot like no other toy. This section will examine a few robots that filled these beneficial roles.

4.1 Toys that Develop Social Skills

Robotic toys, with their ability to think and respond, have been used in reactive and proactive ways to help kids socialize. Having a physical object react like a living creature offers more mental health and social benefits than inanimate objects. Specifically, the Huggable robot improved mental health and social interaction more than an inanimate plush toy and a virtual app using the same character design (Matheson, 2019). Robots have a unique ability to be perceived as living things, allowing them to have social benefits.

Through a robot’s programming and use of sensors, designers can implement life-like behaviours. An example is Shelly the Robotic Tortoise, a robot designed to teach kids to treat robots with respect, see figure 2 (Choi, 2018). Shelly is designed to light up and dance when children pet the shell gently (Pearson, 2020). The lights, music, and movements encourage kids to be gentle with positive reinforcement. Shelly is also designed to turn off the sounds and lights and retract its head, and limbs into the shell if hit or petted too hard (Pearson, 2020). This reaction simulates a real tortoise and provides negative reinforcement for robot abuse. Sensors on the robot determine how it is being touched. The robot's programming uses this information to control the motors and lights to react, encouraging or discouraging selective behaviours in children. The reinforcement leads kids to develop their socio-emotional skills without harming creatures or other humans. The movements also make the robot feel alive and earn the kids' respect, similar to a living creature (Choi, 2018).

Figure 2: Shelly the Robotic Tortoise

Note:(Pearson, 2020)

Robots are also designed to build children’s social skills, especially if they struggle to communicate with friends. Moxie is a robot that encourages kids to socialize by offering activities that develop essential life skills. The approach taken leads the child to positive social interactions. Moxie focuses on good habits, like taking turns, maintaining eye contact, emotional maturity, and being a good listener (Ackerman, 2020). Moxie works by setting goals that develop these skills, often including developing friendships with other kids. Moxie is also programmed to set boundaries by getting tired if a child tries to achieve too much in one day (Ackerman, 2020). Having a toy to guide a child to develop social skills allows them to learn at their own pace. The child learns through experience from an object they treat as a peer. Social robots show massive potential as children's toys as they can shape a child's behaviour by simply reacting, allowing the child to develop at their own pace.

4.2 Toys to Get Children Active

A big part of toys is how they get kids active. Basketballs, soccer balls, skipping ropes and more, older kids use a lot of active toys. Since the covid pandemic, Canadian kids have become less active, with only 28% getting more than an hour of daily physical activity (Global News, 2022). Robotic toys bring new and exciting ways to exercise and help kids become active. Hackaball is an excellent example. As seen in Figure 3, the Hackaball is a programmable ball that can survive being tossed, hit and dropped (Hackaball, 2016). Allowing kids to program the ball through a mobile app enables them to create games to play with friends. By allowing kids to program the toy, Hackaball increases its usability and longevity. Kids can get excited to test their own games, and the infinite possibilities for games can keep them active longer. Applying this principle to other robotic toys shows how they hold kids’ interest for longer.

Figure 3: Kids playing with a Hackaball

Note: (Hackaball, 2016)

Robots can also encourage physical activity in hospital patients. Due to medical conditions and a hospital environment, patients and especially young kids, can struggle to stay active. By introducing the Huggable robot, researchers demonstrated how more interactive toys resulted in more activity in children (Matheson, 2019). The benefit resulted from a toy with interactive activities and games. This feature in many robotic toys encourages children to move more than stationary toys.

Toys can also get kids active in productive ways. Getting a child to put away their toys is a useful task and a common problem for parents. Ranger was designed to help parents with this exact problem. Ranger, a robotic toy box, waves whenever a toy is placed in his bin. The reactiveness of Ranger allowed kids to proactively clean up their toys by providing positive reinforcement (Fink et al., 2014). Ranger’s success leads to the possibility of other chore robots being a way to motivate kids. Robotic toys show great potential, from encouraging physical activity to reinforcing positive habits.

4.3 Toys to Teach Children

Playing with toys and games is a common way for kids to learn. Robotic toys have an educational advantage over inanimate toys since they are coded and built. Allowing kids to see the inner workings of a robot gives them hands-on experience in STEM activities. Therefore, build-your-own robot kits support children's programming skills and ease the transition into computerized coding as they grow up (Metin, 2020). Introducing coding allows kids to build on their computer knowledge at a young age.

Robot kits are one of many types of toys that introduce coding. Other toy designs allow kids to input a series of movements to help a robot navigate through obstacles. The Code-A-Pillar by Fisher Price is an example of such a toy. As seen in figure 4, kids can add instructions to the toy by attaching body segments to the Code-A-Pillar (Fisher Price, 2016). This form of play demonstrates to children how strictly robots follow instructions, starting them on a path to understanding how robots think.

Figure 4: Code-a-Pillar

Note:(Walmart, N.D.)

Inputting instructions is only one way to teach a robot. Through artificial intelligence(A.I.), developers can input a result. The robot determines how to move based on the desired results, forming its instructions. Using the Ergo Jr, Robot kids can train a robot by recording movements. The movements get saved as scratch code, and activating the robot allows the user to play the movements back (Stefania, 2017). This procedure can give kids an easy and understandable way to learn about A.I.

Training a robot through movements can also teach kids about their own movements. For example, training a robot to handwrite can improve the child’s handwriting (Lemaignan et al., 2016). The study told children that the robot needed help learning to handwrite. Kids could move the robot's arms around and have the robot playback what it learnt. This procedure improved the child’s handwriting and hand-eye coordination (Lemaignan et al., 2016). As kids trained the robot, they had to think critically about the movement, which improved their understanding. Applying the principle to other physical movements shows the benefits robots can have on a child’s coordination. Teaching through robots has shown great potential in many areas, from mental skills to physical coordination.

5.0 Conclusion

Robotic toys are designed to solve needs. Robots can be toys that grow with the child or bring one age group the most joy. Adding textures and sounds makes a toy inclusive and supports disabilities. Social robots, like Shelly and Moxie, can improve mental health and socio-emotional skills. Robotic toys, like the Hack-a-ball, can get kids active. While robots, like Ranger, can make kids productive. Learning toys benefit from robotic parts because of their natural ability to teach about STEM and the potential to teach by being taught. The impact robotic toys have made shows their future potential making them a great way to solve problems in childhood.
